**Project Write-Up Draft**

**Research Question and Motivation**
The central question of this project is: *Can a model effectively evaluate the logical correctness of mathematical proofs?* More specifically, I am interested in determining whether a system can distinguish between valid and flawed proofs, rather than simply generating them.

This question relates to existing work in automated theorem proving and formal verification, particularly in areas like Automated Theorem Proving and Formal Verification. Prior research has focused heavily on generating proofs (e.g., systems like Lean or Coq), but comparatively less emphasis has been placed on evaluating *informal* proofs for logical correctness. This project aims to explore that gap by focusing on proof validation rather than proof construction.

---

**Proposed Approach**
To answer this question, I plan to test whether a model can correctly evaluate the logical validity of proofs. The approach involves presenting the system with short mathematical proofs and assessing whether it can identify errors or confirm correctness.

This method differs from traditional formal systems, which rely on strict symbolic logic and fully formalized proofs. Instead, this project focuses on *informal or semi-formal proofs*, which are more common in educational and practical settings. In that sense, the approach is closer to natural language reasoning models than to classical theorem provers.

---

**Preliminary Experiments and Observations**
So far, I have begun setting up experiments where the model is given small, simple proofs and asked to evaluate their correctness. The goal is to start with relatively straightforward examples before scaling to more complex arguments.

At this stage, results are still limited, as debugging work is continuing and other errors are being found. However, initial expectations are that the model may perform well on surface-level reasoning but struggle with deeper logical consistency.

These early experiments may influence how I refine the approach—for example, whether I need to constrain the format of proofs or provide additional structure to improve evaluation accuracy.

---

**Planned Experiments**
Future experiments will involve systematically testing the model on a wider range of proofs, including:

* Correct proofs of varying complexity
* Incorrect proofs with subtle logical errors
* Edge cases where reasoning appears plausible but is invalid

At this stage, no additional experiments have been completed beyond initial setup, as development is still ongoing.

---

**Expected Outcomes and Impact of Experiments**
The results of these experiments will determine whether the model is capable of reliably evaluating logical correctness. If the model performs well, it could suggest that such systems are useful as educational tools or proof-checking aids. If performance is inconsistent, it may indicate that additional structure or formalization is necessary.

Ultimately, the conclusions of this project will depend heavily on empirical results from these experiments.

---

**Challenges and Roadblocks**
One of the primary challenges so far has been determining how to obtain or construct suitable training and evaluation data. In particular:

* It is unclear whether existing datasets of proofs are sufficient
* Creating labeled examples (correct vs. incorrect proofs) may require significant manual effort
* Deciding whether to use fully formal proofs or informal textbook-style proofs remains an open question

Another challenge is deciding how much structure to impose on the input. Feeding raw proofs may be too ambiguous, while overly formal representations may defeat the purpose of evaluating informal reasoning.

These issues will need to be addressed as the project progresses.

---

**Next Steps**
The immediate next step is to finalize the experimental setup and begin running systematic tests on small proof examples. From there, I will refine the methodology based on observed performance and expand the dataset as needed.
